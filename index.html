Can New Knowledge Change
Established Values or Beliefs?

Prompt 11 — An exploration through three historic objects that each reveal a different facet of how knowledge propagates, persuades, and sometimes fails to transform what we believe.

The Exhibition Objects

Three Artefacts, Three Perspectives

01
Einstein's Zurich Notebook, 1912–1913 · Albert Einstein Archives, Hebrew University of Jerusalem
Object One

A Page from Einstein's Zurich Notebook

Tensor Calculus & the Birth of General Relativity

This scanned page from Einstein's 1912–13 Zurich Notebook depicts the mathematical language Einstein used to develop general relativity. The established belief among experts was the Newtonian model: space and time as distinct, static containers. Einstein's work demonstrated that the universe is made of a flexible "spacetime" substratum, affected by events within it.

The expert community accepted this mathematical justification because they possessed the technical literacy to verify the complex mathematical equations. Scientific claims are open to constant revision. When presented with valid proof, this community eagerly abandoned the outdated consensus. Yet for all his fame, Einstein’s actual work remains an complete mystery to most.

Mathematically derived new knowledge changes established beliefs for a literate expert community, while those lacking technical vocabulary remain untouched.
02
Percival Lowell's Mars Globe, 1894 · Lowell Observatory, Flagstaff, Arizona
Object Two

Percival Lowell's 1894 Manuscript Mars Globe

Authority, Desirability Bias & Public Belief

Lowell drew his observations of perfectly straight, artifical canals on this globe, presenting it as proof of alien life. Prior to this in 1894, the consensus was that Earth was the sole planet with life on it. However, Lowell's physical format of the globe carried inherent authoritative weight for non-experts.

A lack of technical expertise prevented the general public from critiquing Lowell's flawed methodology. Their strong desirability bias suppressed scepticism. Official astronomical institutions dismissed the map due to irreplicable data, yet the public readily accepted it as fact.

New knowledge can change non-expert beliefs when a claim is emotionally appealing and presented in a seemingly authoritative format, regardless of its scientific validity.
03
Pasteur's Swan-Neck Flask, 1859 · Musée Pasteur, Paris
Object Three

Pasteur's 1859 Swan-Neck Flask

Germ Theory & the Resistance of Entrenched Belief

The flask's curved neck allowed air inside while trapping dust, disproving spontaneous generation (the belief that microorganisms arose from non-living matter). Pasteur demonstrated that spoilage occurs due to airborne microorganisms, completely transforming the understanding of disease and medicine.

Despite this, 19th-century doctors resisted germ theory because accepting it carried severe moral implications: their own unwashed hands and unsterilised instruments had caused patient deaths. Additionally, a gap in medical education made Pasteur's research incomprehensible to doctors. This ignorance was convenient for their self-preservation. Acceptance of Pasteur's work only began with a new generation of doctors who held different values. We owe our medical advancements today to Pasteur, as without germ theory, modern medicine would not be developed.

Even scientifically rigorous new knowledge may fail to change established beliefs when personal bias and institutional incentive entrench the status quo.

Synthesis

What Determines Whether Knowledge Changes Belief?

Technical Literacy

The Expert Filter

Einstein's tensor calculus changed expert beliefs precisely because physicists could verify it. Knowledge cannot propagate where the language to decode it is absent.

Desirability Bias

The Emotional Lever

Lowell's globe spread belief in Martian life because the public wanted it to be true. Emotional appeal can substitute for scientific rigour in non-specialist audiences.

Self-Interest

The Moral Barrier

Pasteur's flask was rejected because acceptance implied guilt. Entrenched moral stakes and institutional incentive can block even watertight scientific evidence.

Conclusion

The Verdict

Whether new knowledge changes established values and beliefs depends not on its truth, but on who receives it, how it is presented, and what the cost of believing it would be.

Einstein's Zurich Notebook demonstrates that mathematically valid new knowledge readily changes the established beliefs of a literate expert community, but leaves laypersons untouched. Lowell's Mars Globe shows how an unverified claim can transform public belief due to a biased creator (and audience). Pasteur's Swan-Neck Flask reveals that even sound experimental knowledge can fail to change a well-educated community's beliefs when it shakes their personal beliefs and biases.

Together, these three objects reveal that new knowledge is not automatically transformative. Its power to reshape values and beliefs is mediated by the technical literacy of the audience, the emotional appeal of the claim, and the personal stakes involved in accepting it.

Even today, we see how new knowledge, like climate change, is denied by large communities, having tragic consequences.
